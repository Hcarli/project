The Solow Growth Model

Introduction

The Solow Growth Model, developed by Robert Solow in 1956, is a classical model of economic growth that emphasizes the role of capital accumulation, labor, and technological progress. It is used to explain long-term economic growth by looking at productivity, capital accumulation, population growth, and technological innovation.

Assumptions of the Solow Model

  • The production function has constant returns to scale.
  • Output is a function of capital and labor, typically represented by a Cobb-Douglas production function.
  • Technological progress is exogenous.
  • The savings rate, population growth rate, and depreciation rate are constant.

Key Equations

The core of the Solow model can be represented by the following equations:

The production function:

\[Y(t) = K(t)^\alpha [A(t)L(t)]^{1-\alpha}\]

Where:

  • \( Y(t) \) is the total output at time \( t \).
  • \( K(t) \) is the capital stock at time \( t \).
  • \( A(t) \) is the level of technology at time \( t \).
  • \( L(t) \) is the labor force at time \( t \).
  • \( \alpha \) is the output elasticity of capital (a constant).

Capital accumulation is described by:

\[\frac{dK(t)}{dt} = sY(t) - \delta K(t)\]

Where:

  • \( s \) is the savings rate.
  • \( \delta \) is the depreciation rate of capital.

In the steady state, the change in capital stock \( \frac{dK(t)}{dt} = 0 \), leading to:

\[sY^* = \delta K^*\]

Where \( Y^* \) and \( K^* \) are the steady-state levels of output and capital, respectively.

Steady-State Solution

At the steady state, the economy grows at a constant rate determined by technological progress and population growth. The steady-state levels of capital per worker \( k^* \) and output per worker \( y^* \) are given by:

\[k^* = \left( \frac{sA}{\delta + n} \right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}\]

\[y^* = A (k^*)^\alpha\]

Where:

  • \( n \) is the population growth rate.
  • \( A \) is the level of technology (assumed to be constant in the steady state).

Implications of the Solow Model

The Solow model suggests that economies with higher savings rates will have higher levels of capital and output per worker in the steady state. However, it also implies that without technological progress, long-term growth in per capita output cannot be sustained.
